Gnosticism

A technical and historical overview of Gnosticism — its core beliefs, cosmology, key figures, and historical development.

1. Definition

Gnosticism refers to a collection of early religious movements (1st–4th century CE) emphasizing gnosis — direct, experiential knowledge of the divine — as the means of salvation.

Gnostic systems typically distinguish between the material world (imperfect or corrupt) and a higher spiritual realm.

2. Core Beliefs

  • Gnosis: Salvation through hidden spiritual knowledge
  • Dualism: Spirit is good; matter is flawed or evil
  • Demiurge: A lesser creator god who fashioned the material world
  • Divine Spark: A fragment of true divinity exists within humans
  • Revelation: Enlightenment comes via a revealer figure (e.g., Christ)

3. Gnostic Cosmology (Simplified)

Pleroma

The fullness of the divine realm, inhabited by emanations known as Aeons.

Aeons

Divine beings emanating from the Monad (ultimate source). Often structured in pairs.

Demiurge

The ignorant or hostile creator of the material universe, sometimes identified with the Old Testament God.

Material World

A flawed realm that traps divine sparks within human bodies.

4. Key Figures

Valentinus

Influential Gnostic teacher (2nd century CE). Developed a sophisticated cosmology integrating Christian theology.

Basilides

Alexandrian Gnostic thinker who proposed a complex system of emanations and divine hierarchies.

Simon Magus

Often considered an early proto-Gnostic figure, mentioned in early Christian writings.

Sophia

A central mythological figure representing divine wisdom and the source of cosmic imbalance.

5. Historical Timeline

1st Century CE

Emergence of early Gnostic ideas alongside early Christianity and Hellenistic Judaism.

2nd Century CE

Flourishing of major Gnostic schools (Valentinians, Sethians). Composition of Gnostic gospels.

3rd Century CE

Increasing opposition from proto-orthodox Christianity. Writings of Church Fathers against Gnosticism.

4th Century CE

Decline of organized Gnostic movements following Christian orthodoxy becoming dominant in the Roman Empire.

1945 CE

Discovery of the Nag Hammadi Library in Egypt, providing original Gnostic texts.
